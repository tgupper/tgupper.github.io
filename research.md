### Teaching Robots to Play Soccer
#### On Human-Technology Relations in the Development of Software for Humanoid Robots

For my Ph.D. project, I conduct ethnographic and autoethnographic research on the processes through which software for humanoid robots is written. I have joined a team of computer scientists who program such robots to play soccer as an ethnographer, as well as an active member who contributes to the team’s activities, including programming. Through this research, I want to find out how the programming processes, as well as the software that is their result, are influenced by social aspects, by imaginaries of robots and artificial intelligence, and by the technological objects the computer scientists interact with. 

The field I conduct research in allows me to observe a range of technical approaches to robotics software, including neural network-based object detection and autonomous behavior, commonly labeled as “artificial intelligence.” Given that these technical approaches are equally relevant in technologies such as self-driving cars or autonomous weapon systems, my work can also contribute to a deeper understanding of the development of these more controversial applications of AI and robotics.

I find it very important that the results of my project are relevant across disciplinary boundaries, particularly because I also study Computer Science in parallel to my Ph.D. in Cultural Anthropology. This is why I have presented and published in various fields, including Cultural Anthropology, Science and Technology Studies (STS), and Human-Computer Interaction (HCI). I am also convinced that the current societal debate on how we want to live with robots in the future could benefit from empirical insights into how these technologies are developed, which is why I strive to make my results available outside of academia, too.

#### Keywords: 
Anthropology of Science and Technology, Material Culture, Lab Studies, Humanoid Robotics, Artificial Intelligence
